Q1. Water-cement ratio in concrete primarily affects which TWO properties simultaneously — but in opposing directions?

A) Workability and durability
B) Compressive strength and setting time
C) Workability and strength  ✅ CORRECT
D) Shrinkage and heat of hydration

Explanation: Increasing w/c ratio improves workability but reduces compressive strength — they move in opposite directions. This is the classic trade-off in mix design.

------------------------------------------------

Q2. A concrete mix design yields M25 grade. If the cement content is increased by 20% while keeping all other parameters constant, what is the MOST likely outcome?

A) Compressive strength increases to M30
B) Workability decreases
C) Shrinkage and cracking tendency increases  ✅ CORRECT
D) Water-cement ratio remains the same

Explanation: More cement with same water content means more heat of hydration and higher paste volume — leading to greater shrinkage and cracking risk. Strength may marginally improve but thermal cracking can offset it.

------------------------------------------------

Q3. Which chemical admixture, when overdosed, can act as a retarder instead of its intended purpose?

A) Fly ash
B) Superplasticizer (PCE-based)  ✅ CORRECT
C) Accelerating admixture
D) Air-entraining agent

Explanation: PCE-based superplasticizers, when overdosed, can cause excessive retardation of setting — a well-known trap in construction practice.

------------------------------------------------

Q4. IS 383 classifies aggregates into Zone I to Zone IV based on grading. Which zone aggregate, if used in excess, causes the highest water demand in a concrete mix?

A) Zone I (coarser)
B) Zone II
C) Zone III
D) Zone IV (finer)  ✅ CORRECT

Explanation: Zone IV (finest grading) has the highest surface area, demanding more water for the same workability. This is a common trap — students often confuse 'fine aggregate zone' with 'fine aggregate size'.

------------------------------------------------

Q5. Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC 53 grade) achieves its 28-day strength faster than OPC 33 grade primarily because of:

A) Higher C3S content and finer grinding  ✅ CORRECT
B) Higher C2S content
C) Lower C3A content
D) Addition of gypsum in higher proportion

Explanation: C3S (alite) hydrates rapidly and contributes to early and 28-day strength. Finer grinding increases surface area accelerating hydration. OPC 53 has higher C3S and is ground finer.

------------------------------------------------

Q6. In a concrete mix, silica fume is added. Which of the following statements is INCORRECT about its effect?

A) It fills the voids between cement particles (micro-filler effect)
B) It reacts with Ca(OH)₂ released during hydration
C) It increases workability of fresh concrete  ✅ CORRECT (this is the INCORRECT statement)
D) It reduces permeability significantly

Explanation: Silica fume drastically reduces workability due to its extremely fine particle size and high surface area. Superplasticizers must always be used alongside it — a common trick question.

------------------------------------------------

Q7. Alkali-Silica Reaction (ASR) in concrete causes expansion and cracking. Which condition is NOT necessary for ASR to occur?

A) Presence of reactive silica in aggregates
B) Availability of moisture
C) High ambient temperature above 40°C  ✅ CORRECT (NOT necessary)
D) Sufficient alkali content in cement

Explanation: ASR requires reactive silica + alkali + moisture. Temperature accelerates it but is NOT a necessary condition — ASR can occur at normal temperatures. This is a classic trap.

------------------------------------------------

Q8. The slump test measures workability of concrete. A mix shows a 'shear slump' instead of a 'true slump'. This indicates:

A) Concrete is too wet and segregation has occurred
B) Concrete lacks cohesiveness and is prone to segregation  ✅ CORRECT
C) Concrete is too dry for the slump test
D) The test is invalid and must be repeated with a flow table

Explanation: Shear slump (where one side slides off) indicates lack of cohesion in the mix, suggesting potential segregation issues. It doesn't necessarily mean the concrete is too wet.

------------------------------------------------

Q9. Which type of Portland cement should be AVOIDED in marine construction exposed to seawater?

A) Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC)
B) Rapid Hardening Cement (RHC)  ✅ CORRECT
C) Sulphate Resistant Cement (SRC)
D) Low Heat Cement (LHC)

Explanation: RHC has high C3A content and is ground finer — both increase vulnerability to sulfate and chloride attack. SRC and PPC are preferred for marine environments. RHC is a trap here.

------------------------------------------------

Q10. Gypsum is added to clinker during cement manufacture. What happens if gypsum content exceeds the optimum amount?

A) Flash set occurs
B) False set occurs  ✅ CORRECT
C) Strength increases significantly
D) Setting time is unaffected but heat of hydration increases

Explanation: Excess gypsum causes 'false set' (premature stiffening that can be broken by re-mixing) — NOT flash set (which is due to insufficient gypsum). This distinction is frequently confused.

------------------------------------------------

Q11. In Hot Weather Concreting (above 40°C ambient), which measure is LEAST effective in controlling the temperature of fresh concrete?

A) Using chilled water or ice flakes
B) Cooling aggregates by sprinkling water
C) Using white-painted transit mixers
D) Using Rapid Hardening Cement instead of OPC  ✅ CORRECT (LEAST effective / counterproductive)

Explanation: Using RHC in hot weather is counterproductive — it generates MORE heat of hydration and accelerates setting. The other measures directly cool ingredients or reduce heat gain.

------------------------------------------------

Q12. Steel reinforcement bars in concrete derive their bond strength from which mechanism PRIMARILY in deformed (HYSD) bars?

A) Adhesion between steel and cement paste
B) Friction at the interface
C) Mechanical interlocking due to ribs/lugs  ✅ CORRECT
D) Chemical bond between Fe₂O₃ and Ca(OH)₂

Explanation: In HYSD bars, mechanical interlocking (bearing of ribs against concrete) dominates. Plain bars rely more on adhesion and friction. This is often confused in exam questions.

------------------------------------------------

Q13. Fly ash (Class F) used as a partial cement replacement in concrete primarily improves long-term strength through:

A) Its hydraulic properties activating immediately upon mixing
B) Pozzolanic reaction with Ca(OH)₂ over weeks/months  ✅ CORRECT
C) Reduction in water-cement ratio directly
D) Increasing the C3S content of the binder

Explanation: Class F fly ash is pozzolanic — it reacts with Ca(OH)₂ (lime) released during cement hydration. This reaction is slow, which is why fly ash concrete gains strength gradually over time.

------------------------------------------------

Q14. Bulking of sand is maximum at approximately what moisture content?

A) 0% (bone dry)
B) 4–6% moisture  ✅ CORRECT
C) 10–12% moisture
D) At saturated surface dry (SSD) condition

Explanation: Bulking peaks at approximately 4–6% moisture content. At SSD and higher moisture, surface tension effects collapse and bulking reduces. Completely dry sand also shows no bulking.

------------------------------------------------

Q15. Rebound hammer test on concrete gives the 'rebound number' (R). Which factor does NOT affect the rebound number significantly?

A) Carbonation of the concrete surface
B) Angle of inclination of the hammer
C) Type of aggregate used in concrete  ✅ CORRECT (least significant)
D) Age of concrete at time of testing

Explanation: While aggregate type has some minor influence, it's not a primary significant factor. Surface carbonation (which hardens the surface), angle (which changes gravity effect), and age are well-known significant influencers.

------------------------------------------------

Q16. Under the Limit State Method (LSM) for RCC design, the partial safety factor for concrete strength (γc) is taken as 1.5. This factor accounts for:

A) Uncertainty in loading patterns only
B) Difference between lab cube strength and actual in-situ strength  ✅ CORRECT
C) Corrosion of reinforcement over time
D) Thermal expansion effects

Explanation: γc = 1.5 accounts for the fact that concrete strength in actual structures is lower than cube strength due to different curing, compaction, and placement conditions.

------------------------------------------------

Q17. Among natural construction materials, which one undergoes 'seasoning' before use, and why?

A) Stone — to remove quarry sap and residual moisture
B) Timber — to remove sap and reduce moisture content  ✅ CORRECT
C) Sand — to remove silt and clay particles
D) Brick — to remove excess calcium compounds

Explanation: Timber is seasoned to reduce its moisture content (from green ~100% to below 20%), preventing warping, shrinkage, and fungal attack. 'Quarry sap' removal is relevant to stone but 'seasoning' specifically refers to timber.

------------------------------------------------

Q18. Self-Compacting Concrete (SCC) is tested using the L-box test. What does a blocking ratio (H2/H1) of less than 0.8 indicate?

A) Excellent passing ability — concrete flows freely
B) Inadequate passing ability — risk of blocking around reinforcement  ✅ CORRECT
C) Excessive segregation of aggregates
D) Too high a viscosity requiring more superplasticizer

Explanation: H2/H1 < 0.8 indicates that concrete did not flow freely through the reinforcing bars — it is blocking. The target is H2/H1 ≥ 0.8 for adequate passing ability.

------------------------------------------------

Q19. Efflorescence on brick masonry is caused by:

A) Carbonation of lime in mortar by CO₂
B) Soluble salts in bricks or mortar crystallizing on the surface as water evaporates  ✅ CORRECT
C) Organic growth (algae/fungi) on the surface
D) Expansion of bricks due to moisture absorption

Explanation: Efflorescence is the white crystalline deposit formed when soluble salts (sulfates, chlorides) migrate to the surface with water and crystallize upon evaporation. It is NOT biological growth.

Q20. In concrete mix design as per IS 10262, the target mean strength (f'ck) is higher than the characteristic strength (fck). For M30 concrete with a standard deviation of 5 N/mm², what is the target mean strength?

A) 30 N/mm²
B) 36.5 N/mm²
C) 38.25 N/mm²  ✅ CORRECT
D) 41.5 N/mm²

Explanation: Target mean strength f'ck = fck + 1.65σ = 30 + 1.65×5 = 30 + 8.25 = 38.25 N/mm². The factor 1.65 corresponds to 5% defective (IS 10262). A common trap is using the wrong multiplier.

------------------------------------------------

Q21. Which statement about Lightweight Aggregate Concrete is CORRECT?

A) It always has lower compressive strength than normal concrete of the same grade
B) Its thermal insulation is poorer than normal concrete
C) Air-dry density is typically 800–2000 kg/m³ compared to normal concrete's ~2400 kg/m³  ✅ CORRECT
D) It cannot be used for structural purposes under any IS code provisions

Explanation: Lightweight aggregate concrete ranges from 800–2000 kg/m³. It CAN be structural (structural lightweight concrete). Its thermal insulation is BETTER (not poorer) than normal concrete.

------------------------------------------------

Q22. The initial setting time of OPC (as per IS 269) must be NOT LESS than 30 minutes. This minimum is specified to ensure:

A) Concrete gains strength before formwork removal
B) Sufficient time for transportation, placing, and compaction of concrete  ✅ CORRECT
C) Prevention of flash set during hydration
D) The cement can be properly stored without hardening

Explanation: The minimum IST ensures the concrete remains workable long enough for all practical construction operations — mixing, transporting, placing, compacting, and finishing.

------------------------------------------------

Q23. In extreme cold weather concreting (below 5°C), which statement is MOST accurate?

A) More water should be added to counteract slow hydration
B) Concrete should be cured with cold water to slow heat loss
C) Hydration nearly stops below 5°C, so concrete must be protected from freezing and kept warm  ✅ CORRECT
D) Air-entraining agents are harmful and should be avoided in cold weather

Explanation: Hydration slows dramatically and essentially stops below 5°C. Frozen water expands ~9%, damaging the concrete matrix. Protection (insulation, heated enclosures) is essential. Air-entrainment is actually BENEFICIAL in cold/freeze-thaw conditions.

------------------------------------------------

Q24. Specific gravity of cement is approximately 3.15. If a sample tests at 2.8, what does this MOST likely indicate?

A) The cement is of higher quality
B) The cement is adulterated or has absorbed moisture / undergone partial hydration  ✅ CORRECT
C) Normal variation within IS tolerance limits
D) The cement has higher fineness than standard

Explanation: True specific gravity of OPC is 3.1–3.16. A value of 2.8 is significantly lower, indicating adulteration (addition of inerts like fly ash without declaration) or pre-hydration due to moisture. Lower SG ≠ better quality.

------------------------------------------------

Q25. Polymer Modified Concrete (PMC) uses latex or resins. Compared to ordinary concrete, PMC primarily excels in which property that makes it ideal for repair works?

A) Higher compressive strength
B) Better bond/adhesion to existing concrete substrate and improved tensile strength  ✅ CORRECT
C) Lower cost of production
D) Higher resistance to high temperatures

Explanation: PMC's key advantage is superior bond to existing concrete substrates (critical for repair), along with improved flexural and tensile strength. It is NOT cheaper, and polymers degrade at high temperatures.
